Project Background

A welding company has been awarded a significant contract to manufacture 500 structural components using the Gas Metal Arc Welding (GMAW) process. To ensure the project meets the required quality standards, the quality assurance team is tasked with monitoring and inspecting every weld. Each weld must achieve the specified level of penetration, maintain appropriate heat input, and provide sufficient structural integrity.

To support this process, engineers use real-time monitoring equipment such as a clamp meter, thermal imaging camera, Arc Welding Analyzer, and LVDT sensors. The data collected allows engineers to continuously assess welding performance, identify issues early, and ensure all components meet the required specifications.

Key Objectives

  • To understand the basic principles and procedures involved in measurement operations.

  • To measure and analyze important welding parameters during the welding process.

  • To develop hands-on skills in using clamp meters correctly and safely.

  • To utilize thermal imaging technology for collecting and monitoring welding data.

  • To propose suitable corrective actions to improve welding quality and prevent operational issues.

Diagnostic Equipment & Sensors

01 / CLAMP METER

Measures welding current (A) and arc voltage (V) non-intrusively during active arc state for power computations.

02 / THERMAL CAMERA

Captures infrared radiation to map surface temperatures of the weld pool and Heat Affected Zone (HAZ) at ε=0.95.

03 / ARC WELDING ANALYZER

Detects dynamic physical reaction loads (N) resulting from expanding base metal under intense arc constraints.

04 / LVDT SENSOR

Tracks sub-micron linear displacement (mm) to verify final structural geometric shrinkage distortions after cooling.

Engineering Evaluation

The real-time multi-sensor monitoring system applied in the GMAW process proved effective in detecting changes and irregularities during welding operations. The use of continuous process data allows potential welding defects to be identified and addressed at an early stage.

Future Recommendations

For improved operational performance, the use of multi-sensor monitoring technology should be adopted as part of routine manufacturing practices. Furthermore, scheduled sensor maintenance and calibration are essential to ensure measurement accuracy.

Topic 2 — Thermal Analysis

Infrared Detection Principle

Every matter above absolute zero emits electromagnetic infrared signatures. The thermal monitoring system targets a microbolometer array to capture long-wave infrared energy (LWIR, 8-14µm) directly transforming photons into electrical signal variations. Setting the profile configuration to an emissivity of Ɛ = 0.95 matches the surface properties of cooling mild steel.
